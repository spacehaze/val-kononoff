# Outreach email templates

Three templates ranked by warmth: cold (least warm), warm (you've got a real signal), and a follow-up.
None are pushy. All open with **why them**, not why you.

---

**Subject:** quick thought on {{Company}}'s release pipeline

> Hi {{First name}},
>
> I noticed {{Company}} {{specific signal — recent funding round, public engineering blog post, hiring "Senior QA" role, talked about flaky tests at $conf}}. Reading between the lines: there's a quality-engineering bottleneck somewhere between merge and ship.

> I'm a QA & Quality Engineering consultant. I help engineering leaders cut time-from-merge-to-verified-release — usually by 30–40% — through strategy, test automation, and AI-augmented workflows that scale with the team. Background: 12+ years across 8 scrum teams; recently led an org-wide shift to LLM-assisted testing.
>
> Worth a 30-min call to see if it's a fit? Here's my calendar — [calendly.com/valkononoff](https://calendly.com/valkononoff)
>
> Either way, valkononoff.com has the full breakdown.
>
> Val

**Notes:** the `{{specific signal}}` line is the whole email. Replace it with one real sentence about something you read on their engineering blog, in a job post, in a podcast. If you can't fill that line in honestly, don't send the email — pick a different prospect.

## Subject-line rules

- **Specific to them, not you.** "QA bottleneck at {{Company}}" beats "QA Consulting Available."
- **Lowercase.** Reads like a real human email, not a marketing blast.
- **No emoji**, no `[CONSULTING]` brackets, no "Quick question?" (everyone's onto that one).

## Body rules

- **First sentence = signal you've done your homework.** Skip otherwise.
- **One paragraph of credentials max.** They're skimming.
- **Two CTAs, ranked.** Calendly is primary. Reply is the lower-effort backup.
- **Sign off "Val".** Just the first name. Anything more formal makes you sound junior.
